Fantasy Football Week 16: Must-Starts, Risky Sits, and Under-the-Radar Sleepers

Posted on 2024-12-19

The fantasy football playoffs are here, and one wrong move can send your season spiraling. Forget relying on past performance; it’s time to make some crucial decisions to secure that championship. This week’s matchups are pivotal, so let’s dive into who to start, who to sit, and some sneaky sleepers that could win you your league.

Quarterback Conundrums: Daniels and Purdy

First up, quarterback. Are you panicking about Jayden Daniels after a rough game against the Eagles? Don’t! He’s a must-start. Recovered from his rib injury, Daniels has averaged 27 fantasy points per game and possesses the game-breaking upside with his running ability, making him a fantastic play. He also gets a matchup against the Eagles where they have allowed top 8 fantasy performances in half their games. On the other hand, it might be time to bench Brock Purdy if you still have him. With less than 4 fantasy points in two of his last three games, and only 1 passing TD or less in 9 of his 13 games this season, Purdy has become a major liability. Especially this week, facing a Dolphins defense that’s been incredibly stingy to quarterbacks. It’s rough to bench a player that got you this far, but don’t be afraid to move on.

Running Back Revolution: Connor’s Rise and Pacheco’s Fall

At running back, the path is much clearer. James Connor is a league winner waiting to happen. Fresh off being the overall RB1 last week with 31 points, he faces the Panthers, who have allowed the most rushing yards, rushing touchdowns, and fantasy points to running backs this season. This could be another monster game for Connor. On the opposite end, it might be time to let go of the Chiefs Isaiah Pacheco. Since his return, he’s not been the same player, splitting carries with Kareem Hunt, and now with Samaje Perine eating into passing down snaps. With an inconsistent offensive line and a tough Texans matchup, Pacheco is a risky play this week.

Sleepers to Snag: Rush, Stevenson, and More

For those in need of a boost, don’t overlook these sleepers. Quarterback Cooper Rush has been quietly putting up numbers, with eight touchdown passes in his last four games and gets to go against the Buccaneers who have allowed the second most fantasy points per game to quarterbacks. Rhamondre Stevenson is dominating the snaps and touches in New England, and with the Bills struggling to defend running backs, he has the potential for a high upside week, especially in the receiving game. Lastly, Michael McCorkell Jones has actually been a top 10 fantasy QB in 2 of his last 3 and gets a Raiders defense that’s been torched through the air recently, making him an interesting play.

The Bottom Line

Fantasy playoffs are not the time for sentimentality. Be bold, trust your gut, and take the matchups seriously. The right moves this week can lead you to a championship victory. Now, it’s your turn – who are you starting or sitting this crucial week? Share your thoughts and any sleepers you’re eyeing in the comments below!

Disney Edits Transgender Storyline: Art vs. Politics in 'Win or Lose'

Posted on 2024-12-19

Is Disney stepping back from social commentary? That’s the question on many minds after news broke about the animated series, ‘Win or Lose’. Originally set to include a transgender character with dialogue explicitly addressing her gender identity, Disney has now decided to remove those specific lines. While the character will remain, the move has sparked debate about the intersection of entertainment, politics, and social responsibility.

The studio claims its decision is to allow parents to discuss these topics with their children on their own time and terms. That’s a reasonable approach in theory, but the timing of the edit - so late in the production process - raises eyebrows. It begs the question: If this was the creative vision, why did the storyline progress so far with the original intention? Some experts suggest the move might be due to a shift in leadership and a desire to avoid backlash, especially given the current climate surrounding transgender issues and culture wars. This feels like a significant departure from Disney’s recent push for inclusive storytelling in animated films like ‘Lightyear’, where same-sex relationships were part of the narrative.

Perhaps the real issue here is the push and pull between artistic expression and commercial concerns. Disney, as a major media conglomerate, often walks a tightrope. On one hand, it seeks to create art that reflects the diversity of the world. On the other, it is acutely aware of the potential economic consequences of alienating certain segments of its audience. The late stage edit of ‘Win or Lose’ suggests a significant shift in how Disney weighs those competing priorities. It’s a fascinating case study in corporate decision-making.

Ultimately, Disney’s choice will likely fuel the conversation around representation in media and what role entertainment companies should play in shaping societal norms.
