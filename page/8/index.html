Brody, whose latest film “The Brutalist” explores the life of a Hungarian architect in post-war America, shared a deeply personal connection to the story. As the son of a Hungarian immigrant who fled the 1956 revolution, he understands the complexities and sacrifices of the immigrant experience. This background has provided him with invaluable insight that informs his approach to the character, Laszlo Toth. The film clearly has a special place in his heart, as he describes it as an “honor” to bring these themes to the forefront.

So, what exactly do Hungarians and New Yorkers have in common? According to Brody, both are “wonderfully enthusiastic and passionate, and somewhat outspoken.” He further elaborates that both groups share a particular brand of directness, often laced with dark humor. This style of communication might sometimes leave you wondering whether they’re joking or not, yet beneath the surface, there is always a kernel of truth. This bold honesty and willingness to express themselves openly is what he says is a notable trait for both cultural groups.

“The Brutalist” doesn’t just focus on cultural similarities; it also delves into the universal themes of immigration, displacement, and the search for belonging. Brody’s performance, informed by his own family history, adds a layer of authenticity and emotional depth to the film. It also reminds us that stories like Laszlo’s are the backbone of this nation.
