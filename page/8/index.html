Are Pharmacy Benefit Managers Creating 'Pharmacy Deserts' and Inflated Drug Costs?

Posted on 2024-12-19

Have you ever wondered why your prescription costs seem so unpredictable? Or why your local, independent pharmacy suddenly closed its doors? You’re not alone. A concerning trend is sweeping across Minnesota and potentially the nation: the rise of ‘pharmacy deserts’ – areas where access to local pharmacies is severely limited, forcing people to travel long distances or rely on mail-order services. But what’s causing this, and are powerful pharmacy middlemen to blame?

The Middlemen Problem: Who Are PBMs?
Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) are the companies that negotiate drug prices between drug manufacturers, insurance companies, and pharmacies. Initially meant to help keep consumer costs down, these PBMs have become increasingly powerful. Today, three major companies – Optum, CVS Caremark, and Express Scripts – control a staggering 80% of all prescriptions filled in the United States. Here’s the twist: these same PBMs are often part of large healthcare corporations that also own their own pharmacies. Is this a conflict of interest? It certainly looks like it, and critics are claiming it allows these PBMs to prioritize their own pharmacies while squeezing out independent pharmacies.

Independent Pharmacies on the Brink
Independent pharmacies, like the ones run by Deb Keaveney and Deborah Lidl, are struggling to stay afloat. These pharmacies claim that PBMs are reimbursing them at rates that don’t even cover the wholesale cost of some medications. For example, one receipt showed a pharmacy losing money selling a common diabetes medication. This forces patients to travel further, often having to use large chain pharmacies owned by the very entities putting the independents out of business. The result is the creation of these pharmacy deserts, leaving communities without readily accessible, personal, and trusted healthcare providers.

The Fight for Transparency
The good news is that this issue isn’t going unnoticed. The Federal Trade Commission is investigating PBMs, noting that these “powerful middlemen may be profiting by inflating drug costs and squeezing main street pharmacies”. There is a bipartisan bill in the U.S. Senate designed to add much-needed transparency to PBM practices. Minnesota, too, has made attempts to tackle the issue, penalizing PBMs for pushing customers to their own pharmacies. However, these powerful middlemen continue to claim their focus is on reducing costs for consumers, despite evidence to the contrary.

Your Takeaway
The fight for fair drug prices and accessible pharmacies is far from over. We need transparency and accountability from PBMs to ensure that independent pharmacies can thrive and that patients have easy access to their medications without having to drive 20 plus miles. Washington's Juvenile Detention Crisis: Overcrowding, Scandal, and a New Solution?

Posted on 2024-12-19

Is the system failing our youth? That’s the question many are asking in Washington State, where juvenile detention facilities are facing a crisis of overcrowding and scandal. The situation at Green Hill, the state’s detention center for older inmates, has been making headlines for all the wrong reasons, from staff misconduct to rampant drug use and violence. Now, the state is scrambling to find solutions, and their latest move is sparking debate.

The crux of the issue is a stark mismatch between the number of inmates and the facility’s capacity. Green Hill is designed for 180 inmates, but as of recently, it housed a staggering 239 young men. This extreme overcrowding isn’t just a matter of numbers; it’s creating a breeding ground for problems. The result? A concerning rise in drug overdoses, fights, and even inappropriate relationships between staff and inmates. The state has tried to implement new protocols, but their efforts seem to be outpaced by the overwhelming challenges of an overpopulated facility. It paints a disturbing picture of a system stretched far beyond its limits.

So, what’s the answer? The state is now opening a new unit within an adult correctional facility, Stafford Creek. This move, a repurposing of vacant space, is intended to alleviate the overcrowding at Green Hill, adding 48 new beds. While this provides a short-term solution, the fact that it had to resort to using an adult facility for juvenile offenders speaks volumes about the severity of the situation. This move also comes after the department was taken to court for trying to send juvenile inmates to adult prisons, a policy they had to reverse. The state’s actions highlight the challenges in balancing safety and the welfare of these young people.

What does the future hold for Washington’s juvenile justice system? With a new director for the Department of Children, Youth, and Families, the agency responsible for juvenile facilities, there is hope for a fresh approach to fixing these systemic problems. However, it remains clear that a much bigger, comprehensive approach is needed. The problem isn’t simply solved with new beds; it requires addressing the root causes of juvenile delinquency, implementing effective prevention programs, and promoting rehabilitation.
