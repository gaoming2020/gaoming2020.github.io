Are Pharmacy Benefit Managers Creating 'Pharmacy Deserts' and Inflated Drug Costs?

Posted on 2024-12-19

Have you ever wondered why your prescription costs seem so unpredictable? Or why your local, independent pharmacy suddenly closed its doors? You’re not alone. A concerning trend is sweeping across Minnesota and potentially the nation: the rise of ‘pharmacy deserts’ – areas where access to local pharmacies is severely limited, forcing people to travel long distances or rely on mail-order services. But what’s causing this, and are powerful pharmacy middlemen to blame?

The Middlemen Problem: Who Are PBMs?
Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) are the companies that negotiate drug prices between drug manufacturers, insurance companies, and pharmacies. Initially meant to help keep consumer costs down, these PBMs have become increasingly powerful. Today, three major companies – Optum, CVS Caremark, and Express Scripts – control a staggering 80% of all prescriptions filled in the United States. Here’s the twist: these same PBMs are often part of large healthcare corporations that also own their own pharmacies. Is this a conflict of interest? It certainly looks like it, and critics are claiming it allows these PBMs to prioritize their own pharmacies while squeezing out independent pharmacies.

Independent Pharmacies on the Brink
Independent pharmacies, like the ones run by Deb Keaveney and Deborah Lidl, are struggling to stay afloat. These pharmacies claim that PBMs are reimbursing them at rates that don’t even cover the wholesale cost of some medications. For example, one receipt showed a pharmacy losing money selling a common diabetes medication. This forces patients to travel further, often having to use large chain pharmacies owned by the very entities putting the independents out of business. The result is the creation of these pharmacy deserts, leaving communities without readily accessible, personal, and trusted healthcare providers.

The Fight for Transparency
The good news is that this issue isn’t going unnoticed. The Federal Trade Commission is investigating PBMs, noting that these “powerful middlemen may be profiting by inflating drug costs and squeezing main street pharmacies”. There is a bipartisan bill in the U.S. Senate designed to add much-needed transparency to PBM practices. Minnesota, too, has made attempts to tackle the issue, penalizing PBMs for pushing customers to their own pharmacies. However, these powerful middlemen continue to claim their focus is on reducing costs for consumers, despite evidence to the contrary.

Your Takeaway
The fight for fair drug prices and accessible pharmacies is far from over. We need transparency and accountability from PBMs to ensure that independent pharmacies can thrive and that patients have easy access to their medications without having to drive 20 plus miles. Don’t hesitate to voice your concerns to your local representatives. Share this article and help raise awareness about the impact of PBMs on your communities. Let’s demand better healthcare for everyone.
