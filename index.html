Adrien Brody Reveals the Surprising Connection Between Hungarians and New Yorkers

Posted on 2024-12-19

Ever wondered what a Hungarian immigrant and a New Yorker might have in common? Turns out, quite a lot! In a recent interview, Academy Award-winning actor Adrien Brody shared some fascinating insights into his latest film, “The Brutalist,” and his own heritage, revealing some striking similarities between these seemingly disparate cultures.

Brody, whose mother is a Hungarian immigrant, explained that his character in “The Brutalist,” an architect named Laszlo Toth fleeing war-torn Europe, resonates deeply with his own family history. His mother’s journey, along with his grandparents’ escape during the 1956 Hungarian Revolution, became a guiding light for him in the role. He even channeled his grandfather’s pronounced Hungarian accent, emphasizing the intimacy of his connection to the character. This personal touch adds a layer of authenticity to the film, making it more than just a historical drama; it’s a story of family, sacrifice, and the immigrant experience.

So what exactly do Hungarians and New Yorkers share? According to Brody, it’s their wonderfully enthusiastic, passionate, and somewhat outspoken nature. He describes it as a certain “sensibility” that’s not always easily interpreted by outsiders. Like New Yorkers, Hungarians have a directness that can sometimes blur the lines between humor and seriousness. It’s a style of communication where a joke often carries a hidden layer of truth. This shared characteristic highlights a cultural authenticity and a certain way of engaging with the world, a unique blend of honesty and wit.

The film also explores the emotional impact of discovering a past thought lost. In one particular scene, Laszlo Toth is surprised to find his architectural designs still existing from his time in Europe. This moment of discovery captures the struggle and sacrifices made by refugees, showcasing the importance of preserving memory and heritage. It’s a powerful reminder that even in the face of adversity, artistic endeavors can endure. Ultimately, “The Brutalist” promises to be a moving and insightful film that delves into the universal themes of immigration and artistic legacy, making it a must-watch for anyone who enjoys thought-provoking cinema. Be sure to catch it, and perhaps you’ll find yourself thinking about your own cultural background, or your friends from New York!
