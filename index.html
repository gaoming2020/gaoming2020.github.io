Trump Team Eyes GPS Monitoring for Undocumented Migrants: A Solution or a Band-Aid?

Posted on 2024-12-19

Is the U.S. on the verge of a major shift in how it manages undocumented immigrants? It seems so. The Trump transition team is reportedly exploring the use of GPS ankle and wrist monitors for those not currently in federal custody. This move comes as a potential cost-saving measure, with bracelets costing a mere $8 a day compared to the staggering $150 a day for detention. But is this a practical solution, or just a way to skirt around a bigger issue?

One of the main reasons behind this proposal is the sheer number of undocumented individuals residing in the U.S., particularly in sanctuary cities. Reports indicate that nearly 8 million individuals are in these cities. This raises a question: how to manage this population effectively and affordably? The ankle monitoring program could be seen as a way to keep track of these individuals while deportation proceedings are underway. It aims to address the immediate challenge of maintaining a degree of control without resorting to mass incarceration, something the U.S. lacks sufficient capacity for currently.

However, there are serious concerns about the efficacy of this solution. As the recent heartbreaking incident involving the Tren de Aragua gang in Aurora, Colorado demonstrates – where individuals were allegedly tortured – some question whether GPS bracelets are a real solution. It is important to note that, currently, these monitors are managed by private companies, and notifications about a device being tampered with can often be delayed by several hours, rendering the system, according to some officials, “unhelpful.” Furthermore, it doesn’t tackle the root problem and could be viewed as just a stopgap measure. While the Trump administration says they will deport “all of these guys on day one,” they may need a “bridge” to get there, which is where these GPS monitors could come in.

Ultimately, the debate boils down to: is the GPS monitoring system a pragmatic approach to managing undocumented migrants awaiting deportation, or a risky and ineffective band-aid? Rate Cut or Pause? Expert Weighs In on the Fed's Next Move

Posted on 2024-12-19

Is the Federal Reserve about to cut interest rates, or will they pump the brakes? That’s the million-dollar question on everyone’s mind, especially with a new presidential administration looming. Economist Mark Zandi recently shared his perspective on what the Fed might do, and it’s a mix of both immediate action and cautious waiting. Let’s dive into what the experts are predicting.

The Case for a Rate Cut

Many believe that a rate cut is not just possible, but necessary. Why? According to Zandi, the Fed has largely achieved its goals: unemployment is at a healthy 4%, and while inflation is slightly above the 2% target, it’s trending downward, particularly the cost of homeownership. Since the Fed’s mandate is to achieve these goals, and they are close enough, then why wouldn’t the Fed cut the Federal Funds rate to something more balanced? Zandi strongly believes the Fed should cut rates in December or early next year, aiming for an optimal equilibrium in the market. He points out that this is actually pretty consistent with what markets are already anticipating, as evidenced by current 10 year Treasury yields.

The Impending Pause

However, things get a bit more complicated with the incoming administration and its economic policies. The prospect of tariffs, changes to fiscal policy, and potential shifts in tax spending policies create significant uncertainty. Zandi suggests the Federal Reserve will likely want a better understanding of these policies before making additional rate cut decisions. He anticipates a message from Fed Chair Jerome Powell outlining a much slower pace of rate cuts, perhaps even an extended pause to assess the economic landscape under the new leadership. It’s all about making sure the Fed is not jumping the gun amidst all this uncertainty.

What About Jerome Powell?

There’s been chatter about the relationship between the Fed and the incoming President, but Zandi feels this will be a non-issue as long as interest rates are trending lower. He also believes that Jerome Powell will likely address some questions about his tenure in the role. Tensions could rise later if the Fed needs to reverse course and start raising rates again.

The Takeaway

So, what does all this mean? A rate cut is likely imminent, but it might be followed by a pause as the Fed navigates the uncharted territory of a new presidential administration’s policies. The market, for now, appears to be aligned with this prediction, but the situation is dynamic and warrants ongoing attention. What do you think? Recent predictions from Compass CEO Robert Reffkin suggest we might be seeing mortgage rates hovering around 6% for the next two years. But what does this really mean for you, whether you’re a buyer, seller, or just keeping an eye on the market?

The Mortgage Rate Outlook

Forget the hopes of plummeting interest rates anytime soon. The expectation of mortgage rates dropping into the 5% range is off the table, at least for the next couple of years. According to Reffkin, the forecast is for a stable rate of around 6%. This is significant because it impacts affordability and overall buyer confidence. While this might not be the news some were hoping for, understanding this stability is crucial for making informed decisions.

Inventory is Key (and Still Low)

One of the biggest factors influencing the housing market is inventory. While there has been a year-over-year increase in available homes, we’re still about 20% below pre-pandemic levels. This shortage particularly affects the North, Northeast, and Midwest regions. Interestingly, Compass is experimenting with creating more “private exclusive” listings – a strategy they’ve borrowed from builders. By not listing homes on all third-party sites, they aim to minimize negative perceptions, thus encouraging more people to list. While this is helping to create inventory in some areas, we still need to see a larger increase across the board to bring prices down or stabilize them further.

What to Expect in Prices & Should You Rent or Buy?

With mortgage rates expected to remain stable and a modest increase in home prices (3-5% next year, and potentially another 5% the following year), the market is looking relatively stable – just not booming. For those asking the age-old question, ‘Is it better to rent or buy right now?’ The answer might surprise you. According to the data presented in the video, buying seems to be the more economical option. So, if you’re in a place to buy, the market might be more favorable than you think.

The Takeaway
